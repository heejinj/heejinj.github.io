    Dynamic Locomotion Gait Optimization

    Abstract

    Due to its high degrees of freedom, planning dynamic motion that reconciles the full body dynamics of a full robot model such as DURUS has been largely prevented by a particular computational bottleneck: gait synthesis. The focus of my Ph.D. thesis was on developing a computationally efficient and reliable gait synthesis algorithm that is scalable to high-dimensional (more than 20 DoF) humanoids. By decoupling the complex full-order dynamics constraints and exploiting the sparsity structure of the nonlinear optimization problem using direct collocation techniques, this novel framework provides a computationally efficient solution for generating provably stable dynamic locomotion for legged robots. The proposed method notably boosts the speed of the HZD gait synthesis problem by orders of two and significantly improves the reliability of existing methods. This work made fundamental contributions to the formal control methods by removing the limitations of the application of the HZD framework on high-dimensional humanoids.

    By eschewing the need for time-marching integration of system dynamics in favor of local algebraic constraints, this algorithm expresses all optimization constraints in closed-form mathematical formulations whose gradient can be obtained symbolically. By necessity, this algorithm also takes care in addressing the scalability of constraint expression sizes, which can quickly explode to intractable proportions. This is achieved via systematically introducing defect variables into the constraints to avoid symbolically `verbose’ operations to keep the resulting expressions simple. This novel formulation allows for computing the very sparse Jacobian matrix of constraints faster and with higher accuracy. All such components are critical in engineering fast and reliable optimization which synthesize dynamic gaits for humanoid robots.
