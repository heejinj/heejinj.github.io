    Exoskeleton

    Abstract

    Regaining autonomy in their daily lives through the ability to walk again is a central aspiration for many people with lower limb paralysis, i.e. paraplegics. Among the promising solutions to restore locomotion are robotic devices that fit around the legs of a user, called lower-limb exoskeletons for rehabilitation. The existing exoskeletons were able to physically advance the legs of a user, but the user had to use crutches to maintain balance. Throughout the years, in addition to regaining autonomy, clinical tests showed the health benefits of walking for paraplegics: improvement of blood circulation, of respiratory, urinary, and intestinal functions, and positive psychological effects.

    Collaborating with Wandercraft, a French company that builds powered exoskeletons, I am currently working on developing robustly stable, principled control solutions for a powered lower-limb exoskeleton, ATALENTE, to help person with paraplegia to walk without the use of crunches. The exoskeleton has stringent physical requirements: knee cannot be hyper-extended, ankle joints have very limited ranges, and the torso must always be upright. More importantly, the design of an exoskeleton control law will emphasize slow, careful, and guaranteed safe maneuvers that allow the patient to use it comfortably and with confidence.

    To achieve this goal, I seek to develop the fundamental understanding of the necessary criteria for robust balancing that prevents the exoskeleton from falling in any situation, whether it is standing or walking. I utilize my previous experience with dynamic gait optimization to study optimal recovery strategies under different circumstances. For example, under smaller pushes, the exoskeleton can keep its balance while it is standing, whereas the robot needs to take a step to recover if the disturbance is too significant. I gather these data from optimization to design a robust feedback controller for the exoskeleton. This algorithm utilizes advanced machine learning algorithms to understand the underlying dynamic of balancing to develop a smooth multivariate feedback planning controller for robust balancing. More importantly, this work will provide the required guarantee for the safety of the exoskeleton, enables us to develop versatile and dynamic behaviors from standing, to walking on flat ground, to steering around obstacles, to stopping. The ultimate goal of this project is to help a person with paraplegia to walk again robustly and naturally.
